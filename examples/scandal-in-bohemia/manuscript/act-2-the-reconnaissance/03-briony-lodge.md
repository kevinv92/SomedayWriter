# Briony Lodge

At three o'clock the next afternoon a drunken-looking groom, ill-kempt and
side-whiskered, came into the room. It was some time before I could realize that
it was indeed @{Holmes}. He had been out since eight, in the disguise of a groom
out of work, loafing about the mews near @{Briony Lodge}.

"I found @{Irene Adler}'s villa easily enough," he said. "There is a sitting-room
to the front, well furnished, with long windows almost to the floor. Behind is
nothing remarkable, save that the passage window could be reached from the top of
the coach-house. She lives quietly, sings at concerts, drives out at five, and
returns at seven for dinner. She has but one male visitor, and much of him."

"And who is he?"

"His name is @{Godfrey Norton}, of @{The Temple}. A barrister. See the advantage
of a cabman as a confidant! They had driven him home a dozen times from
@{Briony Lodge}, and knew all about him."

As @{Holmes} loitered there, a hansom drove up. A gentleman sprang out — dark,
aquiline, handsome — and rushed into the house. Barely half an hour later he
burst out again, wilder than before, and cried to the cabman, "Drive like the
devil to the @{Church of St. Monica}, and half a sovereign if you reach it in
twenty minutes!"

Then @{Irene Adler}'s landau swept from the door, and she too was gone to
@{St. Monica}. @{Holmes} flung himself into a passing cab and followed.

"I found @{Godfrey Norton} and @{Irene Adler} standing before the altar," he told
me, laughing, "and the clergyman lacking a witness by law. So I was pounced upon,
and found myself mumbling responses, and vouching for a thing I knew nothing of —
and generally assisting in the secure tying up of @{Irene Adler}, spinster, to
@{Godfrey Norton}, bachelor. It was the most preposterous position." %% Holmes,
