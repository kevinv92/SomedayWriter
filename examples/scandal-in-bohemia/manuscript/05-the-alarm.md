# The Alarm of Fire

It was close upon seven when we came to Serpentine Avenue. A group of shabby men
smoked and laughed in a corner; a scissors-grinder ground; two guardsmen
flirted with a nurse. @{Holmes}, in the seemly black of a Nonconformist
clergyman, had a wonderful gift for slipping into a part.

The landau drove up to @{Briony Lodge}. As @{Irene Adler} sprang down, one of the
loafers rushed forward to open the door, and was elbowed away by another. A fight
broke out; blows were struck; and in an instant the lady was in the thick of a
knot of flushed and struggling men, who struck savagely at each other. @{Holmes}
dashed in to protect her — and, at the height of it, gave a cry and dropped to the
ground with the blood running freely down his face.

They carried the wounded clergyman into the sitting-room of @{Briony Lodge}, and
laid him upon the couch by the open window. When he raised his hand, I hurled in
my @{smoke rocket} and raised the cry of "Fire!" The word was scarcely out before
the whole crowd of spectators had taken it up. Thick clouds of smoke curled
through the room. @{Irene Adler} rushed to the fireplace, thrust her hand behind a
sliding panel just above the right bell-pull — and I caught a glimpse of
@{The Photograph} before she saw that it was a false alarm and thrust it back.

"You have it?" I asked, when we had slipped away.

"I know where it is," said @{Holmes}. "She showed me, as I told you she would.
Tomorrow morning we call with @{the King}, and take it while she still sleeps."
